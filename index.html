✦ What is fencing?! ✦

Fencing is a sport where two people (fencers) play tag with metal sticks (blades) on a 14m by 1.5m area (piste). Points are decided by a referee aided by a scoring box which is attached to the fencers’ weapons through wires. The game is technically, mechanically, tactically, and strategically complex.

✦ The weapons ✦

  • Foil

    Foil is often the first weapon a fencer will hold as it is the lightest and was originally intended to teach a fencer the key fundamental skills that will most benefit them in duelling. Points are scored by depressing the tip of the weapon on the opponent’s valid target area.

    The referee decides if points are awarded based on which fencer has the right to attack and whether they hit their opponent on the torso. The fencing is characterised by a back and forth as fencers try to gain the right to attack to score points on a challenging and defended target area. Here are two of the best Timur Safin and Lee Kiefer

  • Sabre

    Sabre is for people who like to pretend they are horses and enjoy galloping at their opponent. The entire blade is valid for scoring points (not just the tip) and the body above the waist (excluding hands) is the valid target area. Sabre is the only weapon where fencers may not cross their legs (encouraging horse-like behaviour).

    The referee decides if points are awarded based on which fencer has the right to attack and whether they have hit their opponent above the waist. Sabre fencing is characterised by attacking fencing as it is greatly favoured due to the large target area making defense difficult with the blade. Explosive footwork is often rewarded. Here are highlight reels from a Women's Sabre Grand Prix and 3X Olympic Champion, Áron Szilágyi

  • Epee

    Epee has the most straightforward ruleset. A referee Points awards points based on whether a fencer touches their opponent anywhere on the body. It is the only weapon where there are no rules regarding a right to attack. It is the only weapon where both fencers can score a point at the same time due to there being no right to attack rules.

    Epee fencing is most often characterised by patient fencing where each fencer tries to create a gap wide enough to score a point while not being hit themselves. There is a lot of testing of each other’s reactions and responses while finding timings. ✦ How did it start? ✦

Weaponplay (sport form of trying to hit someone without getting hit with an object) can be seen in probably every culture with a martial history. Nguni Stick Fighting , Kendo and Kali are other forms of weaponplay which are also still practiced today. The Olympics Website will mention ancient Egyptian depictions of weaponplay to allow fencing to enjoy more history than it can really claim (maybe also to appeal to a sense of universalism). The Fencing which is at the Olympics has a much more recent history.

Fencing as a sport developed out of European weapon based martial arts (these have seen a resurgence due to fencing's more sporty and gamey direction, some people want to learn a martial art, not a sport). There are treatises written instructing European weapon based martial arts from around 1300. The sport was initially created for military training and by the mid-1700s had become an amateur and recreational sport. One figure who aided in this transition was Domenico Angelo. In the 1800s regularised bodies started forming in many countries and by 1896 official regulations were created ahead of the first summer Olympics that year.

The sport’s inclusion in the Olympics is easily what has allowed the sport to grow into one with contenders and champions from all over the world with different backgrounds. Europe has always maintained a strong presence in the sport, most consistently has been Italy, France, and Hungary although pretty much anywhere in Europe, there is a presence. In the Americas, USA and Canada maintain long held strength although Venezuela has done extremely well in recent years. Cuba (much like Britain) have fallen off in more recent years. In Africa, Egypt has made huge strides over the past twenty years. Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia and South Africa also maintain some presence in the sport. In Asia, Russia, China, Japan and South Korea have maintained a high level of fencing since the turn of the millenium. Each of these countries boasting Olympic Champions in the most recent Olympics.

The sport is widespread as a result but remains relatively small in each country for a variety of reasons. The accessibility of the sport is limited as the it is expensive with most people only being exposed to it once every 4 years when the Olympics is on.

✦ What are the requirements for fencing? ✦

Equipment

You should have a blade (epee, foil or sabre), a body-wire (body-cord if American), a mask, jacket, plastron, glove, breeches (knickers if American), and a breastplate should you have breasts to plate. The ESSENTIALS of these are the protective gear. Fencing has maintained a relatively low injury rate because of this protective equipment (most injuries tend to be caused by repetitive strain). Make sure you wear protective equipment and check that it has a badge showing how much protection it offers (usually 350 Newtons or 800 Newtons) or at least feels like you wouldn’t be able to puncture it with a blade.

Body

A body which you inhabit. If you do not have 4 working limbs, look for para-fencing. The main difference is no footwork. Techically demanding blade actions, strong balance with co-ordination and mind-games are all still there.

Competitors

The most important requirement (like almost any competitive sport) is other people to fence against. For most people, this means a club. Almost every club should have equipment and some way to onboard beginners without committing too much money to the sport initially. A club should ideally have a coach, other fencers, and enough equipment to fence with. There should be at least one box with working reels or transmitters.
